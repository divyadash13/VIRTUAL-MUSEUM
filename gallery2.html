Below are a series of images with detailed descriptions.

The Standard of Ur

Description: The Standard of Ur is a hollow, wooden box with intricate inlays of shell, red limestone, and lapis lazuli. It dates back to around 2500 BCE and was discovered in the Royal Cemetery at Ur. The box has two main panels, one depicting a war scene with chariots trampling enemies, and the other showing a banquet scene with musicians and attendants.

Significance: This artifact provides valuable information about Sumerian society, including their military practices and social hierarchy.

The Royal Game of Ur

Description: This is an ancient board game that dates back to around 2600 BCE. The game was played on a board with 20 squares on each side, and pieces were moved according to the roll of dice.

Significance: The game was popular across Mesopotamia and the Near East, and it offers insights into the leisure activities and cultural exchanges of the time.

The Code of Hammurabi

Description: The Code of Hammurabi is one of the oldest and most complete legal codes, dating back to around 1754 BCE. It is inscribed on a diorite stele and consists of 282 laws covering various aspects of daily life, including trade, property, family, and crime.

Significance: This artifact is a testament to the advanced legal and administrative systems of ancient Mesopotamia and provides a glimpse into their societal norms and values.

The Mask of Warka

Description: The Mask of Warka, also known as the Lady of Uruk, is a marble head of a goddess dating back to around 3100 BCE. It was discovered in the temple of Inanna at Uruk.

Significance: This artifact is one of the earliest known representations of a deity and highlights the religious beliefs and artistic skills of the Sumerians.

Cylinder Seals

Description: Cylinder seals were small, cylindrical objects carved with intricate designs and used to roll an impression onto clay tablets or other surfaces. They were used for identification, authentication, and decoration.

Significance: These seals provide valuable information about the personal identities, professions, and artistic styles of ancient Mesopotamians.

The Stele of Hammurabi

Description: This is a large stone monument inscribed with the Code of Hammurabi. It stands over 7 feet tall and was erected in Babylon around 1750 BCE.

Significance: The stele is a symbol of the rule of law and justice in ancient Mesopotamia and serves as a historical record of Hammurabi's legal code.

The Bull Lyre from Ur

Description: This is a musical instrument in the shape of a bull's head, dating back to around 2600 BCE. It was discovered in the Royal Cemetery at Ur and is made of wood, gold, and lapis lazuli.

Significance: The lyre is a testament to the musical culture and craftsmanship of the Sumerians, and it provides insights into their artistic and religious practices.
